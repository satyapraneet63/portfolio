I gravitate toward things that are built, not just imagined loosely — worlds and systems where someone worked out the history, the rules, the consequences, and stuck to them. I don't think I love these stories for their plots so much as for the sensation of a place that holds together: a magic system with real cost, a faction with a real ideology, a political mess that took a thousand years to accumulate. It's the same pull that had me pull apart a 3D printer to understand its control loops instead of just using it. I want to know how the thing works, and I want to spend enough time inside it that I understand it on its own terms.

Games scratch the same itch, just with a lever in my hand — I'm not only observing the built world, I'm making choices that the system responds to.

This list will grow. It's not a ranking, just notes on what stuck and why.

## Fiction

- **The Wheel of Time** — the one I disappeared into longest. Multiple continents, cultures, and magic systems, all with their own internal logic, sustained across an absurd number of pages.
- **Malazan Book of the Fallen** — thrown in the deep end with no hand-holding, a history that predates the books by hundreds of thousands of years and never over-explains itself. Trusts the reader completely.
- **The Stormlight Archive** (and most of Brandon Sanderson) — the clearest example of "magic with rules." Cost and consequence baked into the system itself.
- **The First Law** — same appetite, harder edges.
- **Dune** — politics, ecology, and religion built as one interlocking machine.
- **A Song of Ice and Fire** — history with teeth; nobody's protected by genre convention.

## Games

- **Baldur's Gate 1 & 2, Pathfinder** — rule systems you can feel the seams of, in a good way.
- **Fallout 1 & 2** — a post-apocalypse with an actual anthropology to it.
- **KOTOR 1 & 2** — Obsidian's sequel in particular, for how far it's willing to let a system's own logic turn on the player.
- **Planescape: Torment** — proof that a CRPG's "system" can be entirely philosophical.
- **Disco Elysium** — see [Art](/art) — this one reached past the screen and onto my bedroom wall.
- **Vampire: The Masquerade – Bloodlines** — a whole invented politics of factions and hungers.
- **Warhammer 40K: Rogue Trader** — bureaucracy, empire, and grimdark excess as a coherent system.
- **The Soulsborne games** — the exception to "narrative systems": here the system is the combat itself, taught entirely through failure.
