I don't have a hometown. My parents' jobs moved us often enough that no single city got to be *the* place — no house I grew up in that's still standing in memory, no friend group that goes back to age six, no one dialect that's unambiguously mine. If you ask where I'm from, I have to ask which year you mean.

I used to think of this as a gap — the thing other people have that I don't. The question "where are you from" has never had a clean answer, and for a long time that felt like a small, private deficiency.

I've come around to a different reading. Not having a fixed point means I never had to be from somewhere in order to go somewhere. Moving to London for a Master's isn't a rupture from some settled life — it's the same motion I've been making since before I can remember, just at a longer distance. Every place I've lived, I've had to build my footing from scratch: figure out the new school, the new streets, who to trust, what the local version of normal was. That's a skill by now, not a wound.

So this page isn't a list of cities. It's an ongoing note on what "home" means when you've never had a default one — and right now, the next entry in that note is a flat in London.
